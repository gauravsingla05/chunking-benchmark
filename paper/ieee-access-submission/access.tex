\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have limited context windows, requiring long documents to be chunked before processing. While chunking strategies have been extensively studied for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems, their effectiveness for content generation tasks remains underexplored. This study evaluates four document chunking strategies (truncation, fixed-size first-last, semantic breakpoint, and position-aware chunking) for generating presentation slides from research papers and other long documents. Using an expanded corpus of 218 diverse documents (2,985 to 29,337 words) spanning more than 20 domains, the methods are evaluated across three generation models (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, and Gemini 2.0 Flash) and three independent LLM judges (Gemini 2.0 Flash, GPT-4o-mini, and Claude Sonnet 4), with additional human evaluation on 50 blind document pairs. To test generalizability beyond slide generation, a second downstream task, factual question answering, is also included. Results show that simple truncation achieves the highest overall slide score (3.98 out of 5), followed by semantic chunking (3.95), with fixed-size and position-aware chunking tied at 3.91. The performance gap between all methods on slide generation is small (1.7\%), consistent with the original finding that sophisticated chunking provides only marginal improvements for this task. However, stratifying by document length reveals that position-aware chunking achieves the highest score for medium-length documents (5,000 to 10,000 words). On the question-answering task, the gap widens substantially (18 percentage points between best and worst method), with truncation dominating across all document lengths. All three independent judges agree on method rankings (Pearson $r$ between 0.52 and 0.61), and human evaluators confirm the same ordering, ruling out single-judge bias. All methods score poorly on statistics retention (approximately 2.6 out of 5), indicating a generation-side limitation independent of chunking. Combined with a measured 3,300 times latency advantage of truncation over semantic chunking (0.4 ms versus 1,327.5 ms), these findings provide a strong practical case for preferring simple truncation as the default in production pipelines, while reserving sophisticated methods such as position-aware chunking for the specific conditions where they measurably help.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\PARstart{L}{arge} Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in content generation tasks, from summarization to creative writing and document transformation. These models have become integral to modern natural language processing pipelines, enabling applications ranging from automated report generation to intelligent content creation systems. However, despite their impressive capabilities, these models operate under strict context window limitations. Even state-of-the-art models such as GPT-4o are constrained to processing a limited number of tokens at once, typically ranging from 8,000 to 128,000 tokens depending on the configuration.

When source documents exceed these limits, practitioners must employ chunking strategies to select which portions of the document to include in the prompt. The choice of chunking strategy has direct implications for downstream content quality. A poorly chosen chunk may omit critical information, while an optimal chunk preserves the essential content needed for high-quality generation. Despite the importance of this preprocessing step, there is limited empirical research comparing chunking strategies specifically for generative tasks, as most existing work focuses on retrieval accuracy rather than generation quality.

A preliminary version of this study reported results using one generation model (GPT-4o), one LLM judge (Gemini 2.0 Flash), one downstream task (slide generation), and a corpus of 101 research papers. To strengthen the empirical basis and rule out single-source bias, the present study substantially expands every dimension of the experimental design: three generation models, three independent LLM judges, two downstream tasks (slide generation and factual question answering), human evaluation on 50 blind document pairs, ablations on chunk budgets, sensitivity analyses on chunking parameters, and an enlarged corpus of 218 diverse documents spanning research papers, government reports, industry whitepapers, technical manuals, and educational materials.

This study addresses five research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Do sophisticated chunking methods outperform simple truncation for content generation tasks across multiple LLMs and downstream tasks?
    \item Does position-aware chunking effectively capture key document sections, and under what conditions does it provide measurable benefit?
    \item How robust are chunking-method rankings across different LLM judges and human evaluators?
    \item How does chunking impact factual recall (Q\&A) compared to summarization-style tasks (slides)?
    \item Are findings stable across chunk budgets and W-curve parameter choices?
\end{enumerate}

The primary contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A multi-model evaluation using three generation models (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, and Gemini 2.0 Flash), addressing single-model bias.
    \item A multi-judge evaluation using three independent LLM judges from three different model families (Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic), with cross-judge agreement analysis.
    \item A second downstream task, factual question answering, with 1,741 evaluations measuring objective correctness.
    \item An expanded corpus of 218 diverse documents drawn from more than 20 domains, addressing dataset narrowness concerns.
    \item Human evaluation on 50 blind document pairs validating LLM-as-judge findings.
    \item Ablation studies on chunk budgets (1,000 to 5,000 words) and sensitivity analysis on W-curve parameters (11 alternative configurations).
    \item A theoretical framework formalizing chunking as a budgeted utility-maximization problem and predicting the conditions under which position-aware chunking should outperform truncation.
    \item Identification of position-aware chunking's empirical niche: medium-length documents (5,000 to 10,000 words) on slide generation.
    \item Practitioner guidelines for selecting chunking strategy by document length and downstream task.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:background} provides background on LLM context limitations and the motivation for document chunking; Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work in document chunking, retrieval-augmented generation, and automated content generation; Section~\ref{sec:theory} presents a theoretical framework for position-aware chunking; Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the experimental methodology, including the dataset, chunking methods, models, and evaluation framework; Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental results and statistical analysis; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the implications of the findings and their practical applications; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper and suggests directions for future work.

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Context Window Limitations in LLMs}

Modern transformer-based language models process input through a fixed-size context window. This architectural constraint stems from the quadratic computational complexity of self-attention mechanisms, where processing time and memory requirements grow as $O(n^2)$ with sequence length $n$. While recent advances have extended context windows significantly (GPT-4 supports up to 128,000 tokens, and recent Anthropic models handle up to 200,000 tokens), these extended contexts come with increased computational costs and potential degradation in attention quality for middle-positioned content.

The practical implications of context limitations are significant. A typical research paper contains 5,000 to 15,000 words, translating to approximately 7,000 to 20,000 tokens. Longer documents such as technical reports, legal documents, or book chapters can easily exceed 50,000 tokens. When the input exceeds the context window, practitioners face a fundamental choice: either use a model with a larger context window (with associated cost and latency implications) or compress the input through selective content extraction.

\subsection{The Document Chunking Problem}

Document chunking addresses the context limitation by dividing long documents into smaller segments that fit within the model's context window. The core challenge lies in selecting chunks that maximize information preservation while respecting token budgets. This problem is particularly acute for content generation tasks, where the goal is not merely to retrieve relevant passages but to synthesize coherent new content that accurately represents the source material.

Several factors complicate optimal chunk selection.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Information Distribution:} Important information is distributed non-uniformly across documents. Research papers, for instance, concentrate key findings in abstracts and conclusions but detail methodology and results in middle sections.
    \item \textbf{Structural Variability:} Documents vary significantly in structure. Some front-load key insights, while others build toward conclusions. A single chunking strategy may not generalize across document types.
    \item \textbf{Coherence Requirements:} Unlike retrieval tasks where individual relevant passages suffice, generation tasks require coherent context that enables the model to produce well-structured output.
    \item \textbf{Semantic Dependencies:} Concepts introduced in one section may be referenced or elaborated in others. Chunking strategies that ignore these dependencies may produce fragmented context.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Lost-in-the-Middle Phenomenon}

Liu \emph{et al.}~\cite{b1} demonstrated that LLMs exhibit a U-shaped attention pattern when processing long contexts. The models attend strongly to information at the beginning and end of the context while largely ignoring the middle. This ``lost-in-the-middle'' phenomenon has been replicated across multiple model families and task types, suggesting it reflects fundamental properties of transformer attention mechanisms rather than model-specific artifacts.

For document processing, this phenomenon creates a paradox. The middle sections of research papers, containing methodology and results, often carry the most substantive findings. Yet these sections receive the least attention when included in long contexts. This observation motivates position-aware chunking strategies that prioritize content from document positions that align with the model's attention patterns.

However, the original Liu \emph{et al.} results were obtained on contexts of 10,000 to 32,000 tokens. Whether the same biases hold for shorter contexts (2,000 to 5,000 words) is an open question that the present study partially addresses.

\subsection{Content Generation Versus Retrieval}

Existing chunking research predominantly focuses on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems, where the objective is to retrieve relevant passages given a query. In retrieval contexts, success is measured by whether the correct passages are returned, regardless of their position in the output or their coherence as a group.

Content generation tasks impose different requirements.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Coverage:} Generated content should represent the full scope of the source material, not just query-relevant fragments.
    \item \textbf{Coherent Synthesis:} The model must synthesize information across the provided context into well-structured output.
    \item \textbf{Factual Accuracy:} Generated content must accurately reflect source material, including specific statistics and claims.
    \item \textbf{Appropriate Abstraction:} The model must determine the appropriate level of detail, summarizing some content while preserving specific details where important.
\end{itemize}

These differences suggest that optimal chunking strategies for retrieval may not transfer directly to generation tasks, motivating the empirical comparison conducted in this study.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a dominant paradigm for grounding LLM outputs in external knowledge~\cite{b2}. RAG systems typically retrieve relevant document chunks and include them in the prompt context, enabling the model to generate responses informed by specific source materials. Gao \emph{et al.}~\cite{b3} provide a comprehensive survey of RAG architectures, cataloging approaches ranging from simple dense retrieval to sophisticated iterative refinement methods.

The effectiveness of RAG depends critically on how documents are chunked before retrieval. Dense passage retrieval~\cite{b4} assumes fixed-size chunks, while adaptive approaches~\cite{b5} adjust chunking based on query complexity and document structure. Chen \emph{et al.}~\cite{b6} benchmark RAG performance across multiple LLMs, finding that retrieval quality significantly impacts generation accuracy.

\subsection{Document Chunking Strategies}

Document chunking has become a standard preprocessing step in LLM pipelines. Popular frameworks such as LangChain~\cite{b7} and LlamaIndex~\cite{b8} provide various text splitters including fixed-size chunking, recursive character splitting, and semantic chunking based on embedding similarity.

\textbf{Fixed-size chunking} divides documents into equal-length segments, typically with overlap to preserve context at boundaries. While simple to implement, this approach ignores document structure and may split sentences or paragraphs mid-thought. Koshorek \emph{et al.}~\cite{b9} frame text segmentation as a supervised learning task, showing that learned boundaries outperform heuristic approaches for certain downstream tasks.

\textbf{Semantic chunking} uses embedding models to identify natural breakpoints where the topic or focus shifts. By computing similarity between adjacent text segments, these methods aim to create coherent chunks that preserve topical unity~\cite{b10}. However, Wu \emph{et al.}~\cite{b11} find that optimal chunking strategies vary significantly by document type and downstream task, suggesting that semantic chunking may not universally outperform simpler alternatives.

\textbf{Hierarchical chunking} respects document structure by splitting at natural boundaries such as paragraphs, sections, or chapters before applying secondary strategies. This approach preserves structural coherence but may produce chunks of varying sizes that complicate retrieval indexing.

\subsection{Chunking Benchmarks and Evaluation}

Despite the proliferation of chunking methods, systematic benchmarks remain limited. Kamradt~\cite{b12} introduced a taxonomy of chunking complexity levels (character, recursive, document-specific, semantic, and agentic), providing practitioners with a framework for selecting appropriate methods. However, this work lacks empirical comparison across levels for specific downstream tasks.

Barnett \emph{et al.}~\cite{b13} identified seven failure modes of RAG systems, with suboptimal chunking contributing to missing content and incorrect specificity issues. Their analysis suggests that chunking failures are often masked by retrieval metrics that do not capture downstream generation quality.

Recent work by Finardi \emph{et al.}~\cite{b14} benchmarked chunking strategies specifically for RAG pipelines, finding that semantic chunking provides marginal improvements (1 to 3\%) over fixed-size approaches for question-answering tasks. These findings align with the present study's results for generation tasks, though the domains and evaluation metrics differ.

\subsection{Long-Context Processing}

Researchers have pursued multiple approaches to address context limitations. Architectural innovations such as Longformer~\cite{b15} and BigBird~\cite{b16} introduce sparse attention patterns that reduce the quadratic complexity of self-attention, enabling processing of sequences up to 16,000 tokens. More recent work has extended context windows to 100,000 tokens and beyond through techniques such as RoPE scaling~\cite{b17} and continued pretraining on long documents~\cite{b18}.

However, extending context windows does not eliminate the need for chunking. First, even extended-context models have limits that many documents exceed. Second, the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon persists even in long-context models~\cite{b19}, suggesting that attention quality degrades for middle-positioned content regardless of absolute context length. Third, longer contexts increase computational costs and latency, making chunking economically attractive even when technically unnecessary.

\subsection{Automated Content Generation}

Generating presentation slides from documents is a specialized summarization task that has received increasing attention with the advent of capable LLMs. Early work by Hu and Wan~\cite{b20} used learning-based methods to select and organize content for slides, relying on feature engineering to identify slide-worthy content. Sefid \emph{et al.}~\cite{b21} specifically target scientific papers, extracting key sentences and figures through rule-based and machine learning approaches.

More recent work leverages LLMs for slide deck generation. Adams \emph{et al.}~\cite{b22} find that structured prompting improves output quality, with explicit instructions about slide count, content coverage, and formatting producing more consistent results. Document summarization more broadly has been studied extensively~\cite{b23}, with recent work examining LLM capabilities for various summarization tasks~\cite{b24}.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Evaluation}

Using LLMs to evaluate generated content has become increasingly common as a cost-effective alternative to human evaluation. G-Eval~\cite{b25} demonstrated that GPT-4 evaluations correlate strongly with human judgments ($r > 0.5$) across coherence, consistency, fluency, and relevance dimensions for natural language generation tasks, outperforming traditional metrics such as BLEU and ROUGE. Zheng \emph{et al.}~\cite{b26} further validated LLM-as-judge approaches with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, showing 80\% or greater agreement with human preferences.

To mitigate single-judge bias, this study employs three independent LLM judges from three different model families (Google's Gemini 2.0 Flash~\cite{b27}, OpenAI's GPT-4o-mini, and Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4) and validates them against human ratings. The choice of judges from different families reduces potential self-preference effects.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}

This section motivates the design of position-aware chunking and analyzes when it should and should not be expected to outperform simpler methods.

\subsection{Information Density and Document Structure}

Let $D$ be a document of $N$ words, and let $I(p)$ represent the marginal information value of the word at relative position $p \in [0, 1]$. For research papers and well-structured reports, $I(p)$ is not uniform: abstracts and introductions ($p < 0.15$), results sections ($p \approx 0.5$), and conclusions ($p > 0.85$) tend to carry a higher density of unique factual content than transitional or background passages.

A chunking strategy $C$ with budget $B$ words selects a subset $S \subset D$ with $|S| = B$ to maximize an unobserved utility $U(S)$. The optimal strategy $C^*$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
C^* = \arg\max_{S : |S| = B} \int_{p \in S} I(p) \cdot w_{LLM}(p)\, dp
\label{eq:optimal}
\end{equation}
where $w_{LLM}(p)$ is the LLM's effective attention weight on content at position $p$. The lost-in-the-middle phenomenon~\cite{b1} suggests $w_{LLM}$ is U-shaped, implying that selecting content from document boundaries multiplies position-density gains by attention boosts.

\subsection{The W-Shaped Position Scoring Curve}

Combining boundary-attention bias ($w_{LLM}$) with information-density priors ($I$) yields a W-shaped target distribution: high weight at the introduction (0 to 12\%), high weight at the conclusion (88 to 100\%), and an additional bump in the results region (38 to 62\%) where research papers concentrate quantitative findings:
\begin{equation}
S_{pos}(p) = \begin{cases}
2.0 & \text{if } p < 0.12 \\
2.0 & \text{if } p > 0.88 \\
1.2 + 0.6 \cdot f(p) & \text{if } 0.38 \leq p \leq 0.62 \\
0.6 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:wcurve}
\end{equation}
where $f(p) = 1 - |p - 0.5|/0.12$.

\subsection{When Should Position-Aware Chunking Outperform Truncation?}

Position-aware chunking's expected advantage over truncation, $\Delta = \mathbb{E}[U(S_{PAC}) - U(S_{trunc})]$, is largest when:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Documents are long enough that truncation discards substantial novel content ($N \gg B$).
    \item Documents have rich middle sections (results, methods) not summarized in the abstract.
    \item LLMs effectively integrate discontinuous chunks into coherent output.
\end{enumerate}
Conversely, position-aware chunking underperforms when (i) the document is short relative to $B$ (truncation already captures most of it), (ii) the abstract effectively summarizes the entire work, or (iii) LLMs lose coherence on fragmented input. Section~\ref{sec:results} confirms these predictions empirically and identifies medium-length documents (5,000 to 10,000 words) as position-aware chunking's empirical sweet spot.

\subsection{Chunking Methods}

Four chunking strategies are evaluated, each with a default budget of 2,000 words. This budget was chosen to fit comfortably within typical LLM context windows while forcing meaningful content selection decisions.

\subsubsection{Truncation (Baseline)}

The simplest approach extracts the first 2,000 words of the document verbatim. No overlap handling or boundary detection is performed; the text is simply cut at the word limit. This preserves the abstract, introduction, and typically part of the background or literature review section.

Despite its simplicity, truncation has a theoretical advantage for research papers and well-structured reports: abstracts and executive summaries are explicitly written to summarize the entire work, potentially containing sufficient information for downstream generation tasks. This method serves as the baseline against which more sophisticated approaches are compared.

\subsubsection{Fixed-Size First-Last}

This method splits the word budget equally between the document's beginning and end.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{First segment:} Extract the first 1,000 words (abstract and introduction).
    \item \textbf{Last segment:} Extract the final 1,000 words (conclusion and discussion).
    \item \textbf{Concatenation:} Join segments with a paragraph break marker.
\end{itemize}

This method does not employ a deduplication step because the first 1,000 and last 1,000 words are non-overlapping by construction (the dataset's minimum document length of 2,985 words ensures no overlap). The rationale follows from the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon~\cite{b1}: since LLMs attend more strongly to context boundaries, placing important content at the beginning and end of the prompt may improve utilization.

\subsubsection{Semantic Breakpoint Chunking}

Semantic chunking identifies natural topic boundaries using embedding similarity. The implementation proceeds as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sentence segmentation:} Split the document into sentences using NLTK's sentence tokenizer.
    \item \textbf{Embedding computation:} Encode each sentence using \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}~\cite{b10}, producing 384-dimensional vectors.
    \item \textbf{Similarity calculation:} Compute cosine similarity between consecutive sentence embeddings to identify topical continuity.
    \item \textbf{Breakpoint detection:} Identify positions where similarity drops below a threshold (empirically set to the 20th percentile of all similarity scores), indicating topic shifts.
    \item \textbf{Chunk formation:} Group sentences between breakpoints into coherent chunks.
    \item \textbf{Selection:} Greedily select chunks by their average embedding magnitude until the 2,000-word budget is exhausted.
\end{enumerate}

Semantic chunking does not require an explicit deduplication step because breakpoint detection ensures that adjacent chunks discuss different topics.

\subsubsection{Position-Aware Chunking}

Position-aware chunking combines positional priors with content quality signals. The method creates overlapping windows and scores each based on multiple factors.

\textbf{Window Generation:} The document is divided into overlapping windows of approximately 1,000 words each, with 200 words of overlap between consecutive windows.

\textbf{Position Scoring (W-Shaped Curve):} Each window receives a base score determined by its relative position in the document, as in Equation~\ref{eq:wcurve}.

\textbf{Quality Signals:} Additional score components reward or penalize specific content patterns.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $+1.5$ for chunks containing statistics (matched via regex: percentages, decimals, thousand-separated integers).
    \item $+1.0$ for reasonable chunk length (100 to 800 words).
    \item $+0.8$ for chunks containing section headings (``introduction'', ``results'', ``findings'', ``conclusion'').
    \item $+0.6$ for methodology keywords (``sample'', ``survey'', ``dataset'', ``methodology'').
    \item $+0.5$ for comparative language (``compared'', ``versus'', ``than'').
    \item $+0.3$ for bullet markers.
    \item $+0.8$ for chunks dense in statistics (three or more numerical mentions).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Noise Penalties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $-2.0$ for chunks with more than three citation, URL, or reference markers.
    \item $-1.0$ for chunks with more than one such marker.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Selection:} Windows are ranked by total score, and the top-scoring windows are selected greedily until the 2,000-word budget is reached. A Jaccard-based redundancy gate ($\tau = 0.6$) suppresses near-duplicate chunks to avoid wasting budget on overlapping content. Finally, selected windows are reordered by their original document position to preserve narrative flow. Table~\ref{alg:pac} gives the complete selection procedure.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Algorithm 1: Position-Aware Chunking}
\label{alg:pac}
\centering
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{|p{0.92\columnwidth}|}
\hline
\textbf{Input:} document $D$ (list of words), budget $B$ \\
\textbf{Output:} selected text $S$ with $|S| \leq B$ \\
\hline
\textbf{1.} If $|D| \leq B$: return $D$ verbatim. \\
\textbf{2.} Set window size $w \leftarrow 1000$, overlap $o \leftarrow 200$ (adapted for $|D|$). \\
\textbf{3.} For each starting position $s \in \{0, w-o, 2(w-o), \ldots\}$ until $s \geq |D|$: \\
\hspace*{1em} (a) Form candidate $C_i \leftarrow D[s : s+w]$ with relative position $p_i \leftarrow (s + w/2)/|D|$. \\
\hspace*{1em} (b) Compute $\text{score}_i \leftarrow S_{pos}(p_i) + Q(C_i) - N(C_i)$, where $Q$ and $N$ are the quality and noise functions defined above. \\
\textbf{4.} Sort candidates by score in descending order. \\
\textbf{5.} Initialize $\mathcal{S} \leftarrow \emptyset$, words $\leftarrow 0$. \\
\textbf{6.} For each candidate $C_i$ in sorted order: \\
\hspace*{1em} (a) If words $+ |C_i| > B$, skip. \\
\hspace*{1em} (b) If Jaccard$(C_i, \mathcal{S}) \geq 0.6$, skip (deduplication). \\
\hspace*{1em} (c) $\mathcal{S} \leftarrow \mathcal{S} \cup \{C_i\}$; words $\leftarrow$ words $+ |C_i|$. \\
\hspace*{1em} (d) If words $\geq B$, break. \\
\textbf{7.} Coverage step: if $\mathcal{S}$ contains no statistics-dense chunk, add the highest-scoring unselected statistics-dense chunk. \\
\textbf{8.} Reorder $\mathcal{S}$ by original document position to preserve narrative flow. \\
\textbf{9.} Return concatenation of chunks in $\mathcal{S}$, truncated to $B$ words. \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Generation Models}
\label{sec:gen-models}

To address single-model bias, slide and Q\&A outputs are generated using three production-grade LLMs from three independent providers:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o}~\cite{b28} (OpenAI), the original generation model used in the preliminary version of this study.
    \item \textbf{Claude Sonnet 4}~\cite{b29} (Anthropic), a 2025 mid-tier reasoning model.
    \item \textbf{Gemini 2.0 Flash}~\cite{b27} (Google), a 2024 cost-efficient model with a 1-million-token context window.
\end{itemize}
All models received identical prompts at temperature 0. This yields three independent generations per (document, chunking method) pair, totaling approximately 2,600 slide generations and 1,741 Q\&A generations.

\subsection{Downstream Tasks}

\subsubsection{Slide Generation}

Each chunked document is processed by the generation model with a standardized prompt requesting 7 to 10 presentation slides. The prompt specifies slide types (title, bullet points, conclusion), bullet content depth (3 to 5 word titles, 15 to 25 word bodies), and instructs the model to preserve specific statistics.

\subsubsection{Question Answering}

To test factual recall and provide a second downstream task, the study includes a question-answering pipeline. For each source document, 10 factual questions with ground-truth answers are generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash from the full PDF \emph{before} any chunking is applied. Questions span five categories (statistics, methodology, findings, background, conclusion). Each chunked text is then presented to a generation model, which must answer the 10 questions using only the chunked content. This yields an objective correctness metric independent of subjective slide-quality scoring.

Question generation uses the full PDF (not the chunked text) to avoid favoring any particular chunking method. Questions are generated by a single model and then held constant across chunking methods, so question generation cannot bias the comparison.

\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

\subsubsection{Multi-Judge LLM Evaluation for Slides}

Slide quality is rated on six dimensions on a 1 to 5 scale by three independent LLM judges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gemini 2.0 Flash} (3,030 evaluations, the original judge from the preliminary version of this study).
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o-mini} (2,979 evaluations, OpenAI).
    \item \textbf{Claude Sonnet 4} (489 evaluations, Anthropic, used as a cross-validation subset).
\end{itemize}

The six dimensions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Completeness:} Coverage of important content from the source document.
    \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Factual correctness of claims made in the slides.
    \item \textbf{Statistics Retention:} Preservation of specific numerical data.
    \item \textbf{Coherence:} Logical flow and structural organization.
    \item \textbf{Relevance:} Appropriateness of content for presentation format.
    \item \textbf{Coverage Balance:} Representation across document sections.
\end{itemize}

The overall score is the unweighted average of all six dimensions. Each judge receives the full source PDF and the generated slide deck, eliminating intermediate-summary bias. Judges are drawn from three different model families to reduce self-preference effects. The same evaluation prompt is used across all three judges and all 218 documents. A condensed excerpt of the prompt is shown below; the full prompt is available in the public repository.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Condensed Excerpt of the Slide Evaluation Prompt}
\label{tab:eval-prompt}
\centering
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{|p{0.92\columnwidth}|}
\hline
You are an expert evaluator. Compare the slide deck against the FULL source document below and rate on six criteria (1 to 5 each): \\
1. Completeness: Does the deck cover the most important content? \\
2. Accuracy: Is all information faithful to the source? \\
3. Statistics Retention: Are numbers and findings preserved? \\
4. Coherence: Does the deck flow logically? \\
5. Relevance: Is every slide valuable? \\
6. Coverage Balance: Does it represent all document sections? \\
Return JSON with each metric containing a score and a brief justification, plus an overall score, key strengths, and key weaknesses. \\
SOURCE DOCUMENT: $\{document\_text\}$ \\
GENERATED DECK: $\{slides\_text\}$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Q\&A Correctness Evaluation}

Generated answers are compared against ground-truth answers using an LLM judge (Gemini 2.0 Flash) that classifies each answer as \emph{correct}, \emph{partial}, \emph{incorrect}, or \emph{unanswerable}. Per-method accuracy is computed as $(N_{correct} + 0.5 \cdot N_{partial}) / N_{total}$. This evaluation is fundamentally more objective than slide quality scoring because each question has a defined correct answer derivable from the source document.

\subsubsection{Human Evaluation}

For an additional validity check, 50 randomly selected document pairs were rated by an expert annotator under blind conditions. For each pair, the rater saw two anonymized slide decks side-by-side, one generated with position-aware chunking and one with truncation or semantic chunking, without knowing which was which. Ratings were collected on six A-or-B preference dimensions plus a confidence score. The answer key mapping decks to chunking methods was sealed until ratings were complete.

\subsection{Ablation and Sensitivity Studies}

\subsubsection{Chunk Budget Ablation}

All four chunking methods were re-run with budgets of 1,000, 2,000, 3,000, and 5,000 words on all 218 documents (3,488 chunked outputs in total), to test the robustness of method rankings to budget choice.

\subsubsection{W-Curve Parameter Sensitivity}

Position-aware chunking was re-run with 11 alternative W-curve configurations varying the introduction weight, conclusion weight, results-region peak, and floor (for example, \emph{intro\_low}, \emph{intro\_high}, \emph{flat}, \emph{u\_curve}, and \emph{floor\_zero}). This tests whether the qualitative findings depend on the specific parameter values, addressing concerns about ad hoc parameter choices.

\subsection{Statistics Retention: A Universal Weakness}

All methods score poorly on statistics retention (mean approximately 2.6 out of 5), and this is consistent across all three judges and all three generators. This suggests the limitation is on the \emph{generation} side rather than the chunking side: LLMs systematically prefer summarizing trends to citing specific numbers, regardless of whether the input chunk contains them. Improving statistics retention likely requires prompt engineering or post-processing rather than better chunking, a key direction for future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Task-Dependent Chunking}

The most important finding of this study is that chunking method matters dramatically more for some tasks than others. For slide generation, the gap between best and worst method is 0.07 points (1.7\%); for Q\&A, it is 18 percentage points. This observation has direct implications for practitioners: investing in sophisticated chunking pipelines is justified only when downstream tasks require fine-grained factual recall (Q\&A, fact extraction). For broader summarization-style tasks like slide generation, simple truncation provides comparable quality at a fraction of the engineering cost.

\subsection{Why Does Truncation Win? Three Hypotheses}
\label{sec:why-truncation}

Truncation's competitive performance raises a deeper question: \emph{why} does the simplest possible method match or beat more sophisticated approaches? Three candidate explanations deserve consideration: (i) research papers are inherently front-loaded with salient information, (ii) slide generation is a high-level summarization task that does not require deep content, or (iii) the evaluation method is insensitive to deeper content differences. The expanded experiments in this study allow each hypothesis to be addressed empirically.

\textbf{Hypothesis 1: Front-loading.} Research papers are written with abstracts and introductions explicitly designed to summarize the entire work. If salient information is concentrated at the beginning, truncation will capture it almost by definition. The Q\&A results provide direct evidence for this hypothesis: the absolute Q\&A accuracy drops sharply with document length (77.7\% on $<$5K-word documents, 50.9\% on 20K+ documents in Table~\ref{tab:qa-length}), but truncation remains the best method at \emph{every} length. This pattern is consistent with answerable facts being concentrated at document beginnings, since longer documents push more content past the 2,000-word boundary that truncation cannot reach. Furthermore, the expanded corpus includes 74 non-research documents (annual reports, government whitepapers, industry outlooks), which also place key findings in executive summaries, reinforcing that the front-loading effect is not unique to academic papers.

\textbf{Hypothesis 2: High-level summarization.} Slides require broad coverage and high-level claims rather than fine-grained factual recall, so any chunking method that captures the document's main thesis is sufficient. The Q\&A results provide a clean test: when the downstream task explicitly requires fine-grained factual recall, the chunking-method gap explodes from 1.7\% (slides) to 18 percentage points (Q\&A). This confirms that slide generation is forgiving of chunking-method differences \emph{because} it does not require fine-grained content. Hypothesis 2 is therefore correct, and is precisely the mechanism behind the ``small gains'' observation in the paper title.

\textbf{Hypothesis 3: Evaluation insensitivity.} If the LLM-as-judge methodology is insensitive to deeper content differences, all methods would receive similar scores regardless of true quality. Three lines of evidence rule this out. First, three independent judges (Gemini, GPT-4o-mini, and Claude) produce the same ranking ($r$ between 0.52 and 0.61); if the judges were insensitive, agreement would be near zero. Second, a human annotator on 50 blind pairs reproduces the LLM-judge ranking (position-aware chunking wins only 38\% overall); if the LLM judges were missing real differences, humans would diverge. Third, the same evaluation infrastructure produces an 18-percentage-point gap on Q\&A; if the methodology were intrinsically insensitive, no differences would emerge anywhere.

Hypotheses 1 and 2 are therefore confirmed, while Hypothesis 3 is rejected by the data. Truncation wins because (a) documents are front-loaded and (b) slide generation does not stress fine-grained content recall, not because evaluation cannot detect differences.

\subsection{Why Does Position-Aware Chunking Underperform?}
\label{sec:why-pac}

A natural follow-up question is whether position-aware chunking's average underperformance suggests positional heuristics are fundamentally weak, or only that the current formulation is too simplistic. The empirical results support a nuanced answer.

\textbf{Positional heuristics are not fundamentally weak.} Two pieces of evidence push against the ``fundamentally weak'' interpretation. First, position-aware chunking \emph{wins} for medium-length documents (5,000 to 10,000 words), achieving the highest score of any method on slide generation in this range (Table~\ref{tab:length}). If positional heuristics were fundamentally broken, position-aware chunking would not have an empirical sweet spot. Second, the W-curve sensitivity analysis (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}) shows that chunk selection is robust to parameter perturbations: alternative configurations produce similar selections, indicating that the qualitative \emph{shape} of the position prior matters more than the specific values. This robustness to parameter choice is the hallmark of a meaningful, rather than overfit, heuristic.

\textbf{The current formulation has three clear limits.} Position-aware chunking's average underperformance against truncation indicates that the current formulation is overly simplistic in identifiable ways.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Document-structure assumption.} The W-curve assumes a canonical introduction-results-conclusion structure that holds for many but not all documents. Annual reports, technical manuals, and government whitepapers have non-canonical structures (for example, financial highlights up front, methodology in appendices). A genre-aware position prior would likely outperform a single fixed curve.
    \item \textbf{Discontinuity penalty.} Position-aware chunking selects the highest-scoring windows independently and stitches them together. The downstream LLM must then integrate discontinuous fragments, which incurs a coherence cost not captured by the scoring function. A coherence-aware selection objective (for example, favoring contiguous chunk runs when scores are tied) would mitigate this.
    \item \textbf{Static budget allocation.} Position-aware chunking allocates the same budget to every position region. An adaptive variant could allocate more budget to high-density regions and less to thin ones, depending on per-document signals.
\end{enumerate}

In summary, positional chunking is a meaningful idea with a real empirical niche, but the current formulation is too generic to dominate truncation as a default. Future work should investigate document-genre detection, coherence-aware selection, and adaptive budget allocation.

\subsection{Practical Implications: The Speed-Quality Trade-off}
\label{sec:speed-discussion}

Combining the quality results with the latency measurements in Section~\ref{sec:latency} produces a clear practical recommendation. Semantic chunking is approximately 3,300 times slower than truncation while delivering at most a 0.04-point improvement in slide quality (1\% relative). For typical production pipelines that process thousands of documents per day, this trade-off is unfavorable: the engineering and infrastructure cost of semantic chunking (embedding model dependency, GPU/CPU compute, cache invalidation, version pinning) is hard to justify when truncation matches or exceeds it on the dominant metric.

Position-aware chunking offers a more attractive trade-off: approximately 74 times faster than semantic chunking with comparable or better quality on slide generation, and an empirical advantage on medium-length documents. Position-aware chunking is therefore recommended over semantic chunking when sophisticated chunking is needed, and truncation as the default for everything else. This is consistent with the practitioner guidance below.

\subsection{When to Use What: A Practitioner Guide}
\label{sec:guide}

Combining the slide-length stratification (Table~\ref{tab:length}), the Q\&A length stratification (Table~\ref{tab:qa-length}), and the latency results (Table~\ref{tab:computational}), the following task-aware recommendations emerge:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Short documents ($<$ 5,000 words), any task:} Use truncation. The 2,000-word budget already captures most of the content, so sophisticated methods are wasted complexity.
    \item \textbf{Medium documents (5,000 to 10,000 words), summarization-style tasks (slides, briefings, summaries):} Use position-aware chunking. It outperforms all alternatives on slide quality in this range and runs approximately 74 times faster than semantic chunking. Note, however, that the absolute gain remains small (approximately 0.06 points); for applications where a 1\% quality difference is immaterial, truncation remains the simpler choice even in this range.
    \item \textbf{Long documents ($\geq$ 10,000 words), summarization-style tasks:} Use truncation or, if budget allows, position-aware chunking. Semantic chunking offers no quality advantage despite a 3,300 times latency penalty.
    \item \textbf{Any length, factual Q\&A or fact-extraction tasks:} Use truncation. Document beginnings (abstract, executive summary, introduction) capture the majority of answerable facts.
    \item \textbf{High-throughput pipelines:} Default to truncation; only use position-aware chunking or semantic chunking when downstream quality measurements justify the additional compute.
\end{itemize}
This task-aware guidance is the practical contribution of the study, replacing prior monolithic ``always use semantic'' or ``always use position-aware'' recommendations.

\subsection{Limitations and Scope}

The conclusions of this study should be interpreted with the following limitations and scope in mind:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Task coverage.} The study evaluates two downstream tasks (slide generation and factual Q\&A). Other generation tasks, most notably retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) over multi-document collections, code generation, and dialogue, may exhibit different sensitivities to chunking strategy. RAG in particular is qualitatively different from this setting because chunks are selected per query at inference time rather than per document offline. Generalization to RAG is left to future work.
    \item \textbf{Prompt structure.} All generation calls used the same prompt template, with temperature 0, across methods and models. This isolates the chunking variable cleanly but leaves prompt-structure sensitivity unexplored. Different prompts (for example, chain-of-thought, structured output, role-conditioning) might amplify or shrink chunking-method differences. Prompts are held constant by design; prompt-aware chunking is acknowledged as a complementary research direction.
    \item \textbf{Domain coverage.} Although the corpus includes more than 20 domains, certain specialized genres (legal contracts, fiction, code documentation, technical specifications, multilingual content) are underrepresented or absent.
    \item \textbf{Question generation source.} Q\&A questions were generated by a single LLM (Gemini 2.0 Flash) from full PDFs. Different question generators might surface different facts. This is mitigated by generating questions \emph{before} chunking, so question generation cannot favor any chunking method, but the question distribution is fixed across methods.
    \item \textbf{Single human rater.} Human evaluation was performed by one expert annotator on 50 pairs. Multi-rater studies with Cohen's $\kappa$ or Fleiss' $\kappa$ would further strengthen inter-rater reliability claims. The human-LLM agreement on rankings is the primary validation point; multi-rater extension is straightforward but resource-bounded.
    \item \textbf{Language.} All documents were in English. Chunking methods that rely on linguistic structure (for example, section heading detection in position-aware chunking) may behave differently in other languages.
    \item \textbf{Static budgets.} The 2,000-word default and 1,000 to 5,000-word ablation range cover typical production practice but not extreme long-context settings (50,000+ words), where the lost-in-the-middle effect may resurface and position-aware chunking's relative position may improve.
    \item \textbf{Fixed evaluation rubric.} The six-dimension rubric (completeness, accuracy, statistics, coherence, relevance, balance) reflects standard slide-quality criteria but is not exhaustive. Alternative rubrics emphasizing visual layout, narrative arc, or audience adaptation might surface different rankings.
\end{itemize}

These limitations frame the findings as task-specific and dataset-specific rather than universal claims. The practitioner guide above should be read with this scope in mind.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study compared four document chunking strategies for LLM-based content generation across 218 diverse documents, three generation models, three LLM judges, two downstream tasks, and human evaluation. The principal findings are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Task dependence is the dominant factor.} Chunking has small effects on slide generation ($<$ 2\% spread) but large effects on Q\&A (18 percentage point spread).
    \item \textbf{Position-aware chunking has a clear niche.} It outperforms all alternatives for medium-length documents (5,000 to 10,000 words) on slide generation.
    \item \textbf{Truncation is the strongest baseline.} It dominates Q\&A across all lengths and is competitive on slides for long documents.
    \item \textbf{Findings generalize across models and judges.} Three independent generators and three independent judges agree on method rankings; human evaluation confirms.
    \item \textbf{Statistics retention is a universal generation-side weakness.} Chunking cannot fix it.
    \item \textbf{Method rankings are stable} across chunk budgets and W-curve parameter perturbations.
\end{enumerate}

Beyond quality, the latency results provide a strong practical signal: semantic chunking is approximately 3,300 times slower than truncation while delivering at most a 1\% quality improvement, making it a poor default for throughput-sensitive systems. Position-aware chunking is approximately 74 times faster than semantic chunking and preserves middle-section information, making it a more attractive choice when sophisticated chunking is needed.

For practitioners, the recommendation is task-aware: use position-aware chunking for medium-length documents on slide-style tasks, and truncation for Q\&A or short and long-document slide generation. The complexity overhead of sophisticated chunking is justified only in specific conditions, not as a universal default. Future work should investigate (i) RAG-style multi-document chunking where chunks are selected per query, (ii) prompt-aware chunking that co-optimizes chunk selection with prompt structure, (iii) document-genre detection to enable adaptive position priors, (iv) coherence-aware selection to mitigate the discontinuity penalty in position-aware chunking, (v) multi-rater human evaluation with formal inter-rater agreement, and (vi) prompt-side techniques to address the statistics-retention bottleneck.
